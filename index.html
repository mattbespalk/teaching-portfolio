Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is built on the idea that students learn best by actively engaging with real systems. In technical fields such as electronics, communications, microcontrollers, and cybersecurity, understanding comes from building, testing, failing, troubleshooting, and improving.

My approach is strongly influenced by three key educational role models: Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin. Each of these educators contributed in unique ways to how complex ideas are taught, communicated, and experienced—and each has shaped specific elements of my instructional practice.

Teaching Role Models and Their Contributions

Richard Feynman (1918–1988) was a Nobel Prize-winning physicist known for his ability to explain difficult concepts in simple, intuitive ways...

What I implement: I begin with simple analogies and mental models before introducing technical detail.

Fred Rogers (1928–2003), creator of Mister Rogers’ Neighborhood, was an educator focused on emotional intelligence, safety, and respect. His work demonstrated that learning environments must be psychologically safe and supportive for learners to succeed.

What I implement: I prioritize clarity, patience, and respect in the classroom. I create an environment where students feel comfortable asking questions, making mistakes, and developing confidence in their abilities.

Walter Lewin (1936– ) is a physicist and professor known for his highly engaging, demonstration-driven lectures at MIT. His teaching style emphasized energy, visualization, and memorable experiences that make learning impactful and lasting.

What I implement: I incorporate dynamic demonstrations, real-world examples, and enthusiasm to make lessons engaging and memorable. I aim to create moments that students will remember long after the class is over.

Integrated Teaching Approach

I combine these influences into a structured instructional approach:

  • Begin with intuitive, analogy-driven explanations (Feynman)
  • Create an engaging and memorable learning experience through demonstration and visualization (Lewin)
  • Maintain a safe, respectful, and supportive learning environment (Rogers)
  • Reinforce concepts through hands-on labs and real system interaction

I also view teaching as a communication system. Concepts must be clearly encoded, transmitted through accessible learning experiences, and received by students in ways that make sense to them. My role is to reduce "noise"—barriers such as confusion, anxiety, or unclear instruction—so that learning can occur effectively.

Ultimately, my goal is to develop confident, capable technologists who can think critically, adapt to unfamiliar problems, and apply their knowledge in real-world environments.
