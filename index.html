Competency 1

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is built on the idea that students learn best by actively engaging with real systems. In technical fields such as electronics, communications, microcontrollers, and cybersecurity, understanding comes from building, testing, failing, troubleshooting, and improving.

My approach is strongly influenced by three key educational role models: Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin. Each has shaped specific elements of my instructional practice.

Teaching Role Models

Richard Feynman

Richard Feynman was a Nobel Prize-winning physicist known as "The Great Explainer." He demonstrated that true understanding is not shown through complexity, but through the ability to explain ideas simply and clearly.

At the core of his approach was clarity through simplification. He relied heavily on analogies, real-world examples, and storytelling rather than memorization. He encouraged questioning and openly acknowledged confusion, modeling that uncertainty is a natural part of learning.

Applying Feynman's approach reinforces the importance of deep understanding over surface-level knowledge. Using analogies connects new information to what students already know, improving retention. Encouraging questions helps students take ownership of their learning.

Fred Rogers

Fred Rogers demonstrated that effective teaching is not about delivering information quickly, but about creating an environment where learners feel safe, respected, and able to process ideas at their own pace.

At the core of Rogers' approach was intentional, thoughtful communication: speaking clearly, allowing time for reflection, and maintaining consistent routines. He placed a strong emphasis on emotional safety, acknowledging that learners bring feelings and anxieties into the learning environment.

Applying Rogers' approach reinforces the importance of clarity, structure, and empathy. Creating a supportive and predictable environment reduces anxiety and allows students to engage more fully—especially adult learners returning to education with varying levels of confidence.

Walter Lewin

Walter Lewin taught at MIT for approximately 43 years. Where Feynman made ideas clear through explanation, Lewin made them visible through action—earning him the title of the "Great Demonstrator."

I have adopted his use of demonstrations and hands-on activities to support learning. In electronics courses, this includes building circuits, testing signals, and troubleshooting systems in real time. I also incorporate prediction-based learning: before a demonstration I ask students to anticipate what will happen, encouraging active engagement and critical thinking.

Overall, incorporating Lewin's style allows me to move beyond purely theoretical instruction and create a more interactive, practical learning experience that connects theory to application.

Reflection

My teaching philosophy is the result of integrating the approaches of Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin into a cohesive instructional framework.

Feynman’s emphasis on clarity through simplification shapes how I introduce new concepts. I aim to reduce complexity without removing meaning, using analogies and structured explanations to help students build genuine understanding rather than rely on memorization. This approach ensures that students can explain ideas themselves, which is a key indicator of mastery.

Overall, my instruction balances structure with flexibility, allowing me to deliver clear content while adapting to student needs and supporting ongoing engagement.

Rogers’ influence is evident in the learning environment I strive to create. Technical content can be intimidating, particularly for students who lack confidence, so I prioritize a classroom culture that is respectful, predictable, and supportive. This allows students to engage with challenging material without unnecessary anxiety.

Lewin’s impact is seen in how I deliver content through demonstration and hands-on experience. By asking students to make predictions and then observe real outcomes, I create opportunities for active learning that connect theory to practice.

Together, these approaches allow me to balance clarity, environment, and experience—supporting students not just in learning content, but in developing confidence and the ability to apply their knowledge.

Competency 2

Instruction & Delivery

Effective delivery depends on how clearly information is structured, presented, and understood. I focus on multiple methods—visual, auditory, and hands-on—to support different learning styles and connect theory to practice.

Reflection

Over the past six years, my approach to instruction and delivery has evolved significantly through experience teaching electronics, communications, RF systems, microcontrollers, and cybersecurity in both traditional and highly constrained learning environments. One of the most influential periods in this development was the transition to online delivery during the COVID-19 pandemic, where I was required to rethink how hands-on technical education could be delivered effectively outside of a physical lab environment.

Teaching electronics and RF concepts online presented a unique challenge because these subjects are fundamentally experiential. Students do not fully understand concepts such as voltage, signal propagation, modulation, or circuit troubleshooting through theory alone. They learn by building circuits, making mistakes, testing systems, and observing real-world behavior. During COVID, I had to find ways to preserve this practical learning experience while students were isolated from campus labs and equipment.

To address this, I developed a blended instructional approach that combined physical hardware kits, real-world laboratory activities, and interactive simulation tools. Students worked with actual electronic components at home whenever possible, including breadboards, resistors, sensors, Arduinos, and communication modules. Rather than reducing the practical component of the program, I adapted labs so students could continue building and troubleshooting real circuits remotely. This reinforced the importance of hands-on learning even in an online environment.

At the same time, I increasingly integrated simulation platforms such as PHET, Falstad, Tinkercad, and other circuit visualization tools into my teaching. These simulators became more than substitutes for physical labs; they evolved into instructional tools that allowed students to safely experiment, visualize abstract concepts, and explore systems without fear of damaging equipment. Students could instantly observe the effects of changing resistance, voltage, timing, or logic states in ways that are often difficult to demonstrate in a traditional classroom alone.

Over time, I recognized that simulations and physical labs each provide unique educational benefits. Simulators allow rapid experimentation, visualization, and conceptual exploration, while real hardware teaches troubleshooting, wiring practices, measurement techniques, and the realities of imperfect physical systems. My instructional delivery has evolved into intentionally combining both approaches rather than treating one as secondary to the other.

As a result, my classrooms and labs have become more interactive, student-centered, and exploration-driven. I now place greater emphasis on guided experimentation, troubleshooting, iterative design, and discovery-based learning. Whether students are building line-following robots, analyzing RF systems with a NanoVNA, programming microcontrollers, or experimenting with wireless communication systems, my goal is to create learning environments where theory and practical application continuously reinforce one another.

Looking back, the challenges of online delivery during COVID accelerated the development of my instructional approach. It forced me to become more flexible, more creative, and more intentional in how I design learning experiences. Most importantly, it reinforced my belief that effective technical education is not simply about delivering information, but about creating opportunities for students to actively engage with systems, experiment safely, solve problems, and develop confidence through hands-on experience.

Competency 3

Assessment & Evaluation

My assessment philosophy focuses on evaluating both understanding and process. I design assessments that are authentic, clearly aligned with learning outcomes, and reflective of real-world technical problem-solving.

Reflection

One of the largest changes affecting assessment and evaluation in recent years has been the rapid growth of artificial intelligence tools and their use by students when completing academic work. This shift has required me to rethink how I assess student understanding, particularly in technical and hands-on courses where it is important to evaluate not only completed work, but also a student’s ability to explain, troubleshoot, and apply concepts independently.

As a result, my assessment strategies have evolved significantly. In my electronics, RF, and technology courses, many labs have shifted away from heavily report-based assessments and toward guided, hands-on learning activities that students complete directly in the lab environment. These labs are often structured more as competency-based or pass/fail activities focused on successful system operation, troubleshooting, and practical demonstration of skills.

A major change in my assessment approach has been the addition of instructor sign-offs throughout laboratory activities. During these checkpoints, students must physically demonstrate the operation of their systems and verbally explain how the circuit, program, or communication system functions. This allows me to directly evaluate student understanding, technical reasoning, troubleshooting ability, and engagement with the material in real time.

I have also reduced the emphasis on take-home reflection questions and written assessment sections within labs. Instead, many of these evaluation components have been moved into in-class, proctored quizzes and assessments where students must independently explain concepts, analyze systems, and apply technical knowledge without outside assistance. This change helps ensure that assessments more accurately reflect student understanding rather than the output of AI tools or external resources.

At the same time, I recognize that AI and modern technologies are becoming part of the professional and educational landscape. Rather than treating technology itself as the problem, I believe assessment design must evolve to focus more heavily on demonstration, application, communication, troubleshooting, and authentic technical performance. My goal is to create assessments that encourage learning, reinforce practical competency, and accurately measure a student’s understanding of the systems they are working with.

These changes have also reinforced the importance of continuous observation and interaction during labs and classroom activities. By engaging students in discussion, guided troubleshooting, demonstrations, and real-time questioning, I am better able to assess both technical competency and conceptual understanding while supporting students throughout the learning process.

Looking forward, I expect assessment and evaluation practices will continue to evolve alongside emerging technologies. My goal is to continue refining assessment methods that are fair, practical, industry-relevant, and focused on authentic demonstration of student learning and technical ability.

Competency 4

Diversity & Inclusion

I design my courses to reduce barriers and support diverse learners through Universal Design for Learning. My goal is to create environments where students can access content, engage meaningfully, and demonstrate understanding in multiple ways.

Reflection

Over the past several years, my understanding of diversity and inclusion in education has evolved significantly through both professional experience and personal reflection. As someone who experienced common learning disabilities and struggled within traditional educational environments, I understand firsthand how difficult learning can become when instruction is rigid, overly standardized, or disconnected from the needs of individual students.

These experiences have strongly influenced my approach to teaching. Rather than expecting every student to learn in the same way, my goal is to create an environment where students can use the tools, strategies, and learning methods that work best for them. I have increasingly focused on implementing Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles to reduce barriers and improve accessibility for all learners while still maintaining high academic standards.

In my electronics, RF, and technology classes, this has meant providing multiple methods of instruction and engagement. Students are supported through a combination of hands-on labs, guided demonstrations, written instructions, visual diagrams, video walkthroughs, simulations, and real-world examples. I recognize that students process information differently, and I aim to provide multiple pathways for understanding technical concepts.

I have also worked to create learning environments where students feel comfortable asking questions, making mistakes, and supporting one another. Teamwork and peer collaboration are strongly encouraged in my classrooms and labs because technical learning is often strengthened through discussion, troubleshooting, and shared problem solving. I believe students learn best when they feel respected, supported, and safe participating in the learning process.

My growth in applying diversity and inclusion has also involved becoming more proactive in assessment and course design. I now place greater emphasis on clear expectations, structured rubrics, scaffolded assignments, and flexibility in how students demonstrate their understanding. These strategies help reduce unnecessary barriers and allow students to focus on learning rather than simply navigating the educational process itself.

The integration of UDL principles into my teaching practice has become a major focus of my professional development and instructional philosophy. This growth is reflected in my collaboration with Accessibility Services and participation in the UDL pilot project within the Computer Automated Systems Technology program. The work has reinforced my belief that inclusive teaching does not lower standards; instead, it creates more opportunities for students to succeed and demonstrate their abilities. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Looking back, my own struggles as a learner have become one of the greatest strengths in my teaching practice. They have helped me develop empathy, flexibility, and a stronger understanding of how students experience learning challenges. My goal is to continue building classrooms that are engaging, accessible, technically rigorous, and supportive of the diverse needs and strengths that students bring to the learning environment.

Competency 6

Curriculum Development

My approach to curriculum development focuses on clear outcomes, logical learning progression, accessible instructions, and alignment between learning activities, assessments, and industry expectations.

Reflection

My involvement in curriculum development has primarily been through a supportive and implementation-focused role rather than leading official learning outcome development. While I have not been the primary author of course learning outcomes, I have contributed significantly to the practical delivery, modernization, and refinement of curriculum within the electronics and technology programs I teach.

Over the past several years, I have focused on developing instructional materials, hands-on labs, simulations, assessments, and interactive learning resources that help translate curriculum outcomes into meaningful learning experiences for students. Much of my work has involved bridging the gap between theoretical concepts and practical application through the use of real-world electronics systems, RF demonstrations, guided troubleshooting activities, and technology-enhanced learning tools.

One area of significant growth has been the integration of modern educational technologies into curriculum delivery. I have developed lessons and support materials using tools such as PhET Interactive Simulations, Falstad Circuit Simulator, Nearpod, and multimedia instructional videos created with OBS Studio. These resources have helped improve student engagement, support multiple learning styles, and increase accessibility, particularly during online and blended learning periods.

I have also contributed to curriculum improvement through assessment and lab development. This includes creating hands-on electronics projects, troubleshooting activities, interactive RF lessons, structured lab exercises, and industry-aligned assessment rubrics that reinforce both technical understanding and practical competency. My focus has been on helping students connect classroom theory to authentic technical skills and real-world applications.

Another important aspect of my curriculum-related growth has been adapting instructional materials to support Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles. By integrating simulations, guided demonstrations, scaffolded assignments, videos, and multiple forms of content delivery, I have worked to reduce barriers and create more inclusive learning experiences while maintaining strong academic and technical standards.

I am also currently part of a working group involved in rebuilding and modernizing our programs. This experience has provided additional insight into the complexity of curriculum design, program structure, industry alignment, and balancing technical depth with accessibility for students. Participating in these discussions has strengthened my understanding of how curriculum decisions affect both student learning experiences and long-term program outcomes.

Through these experiences, I have developed a greater appreciation for the collaborative nature of curriculum development in post-secondary education. Looking forward, I hope to continue expanding my contributions by supporting modernization efforts, developing new hands-on learning resources, integrating emerging technologies, and helping create learning experiences that better connect theory, practical skills, and industry expectations.

Competency 7

Technology

I integrate technology purposefully to improve engagement, support different learning styles, and connect classroom learning to real technical systems. Technology is selected based on its ability to improve learning, not simply because it is new.

Reflection

The integration of technology into education has become one of the strongest and fastest-growing areas of my teaching practice. Throughout my career, I have continually worked to improve my ability to use technology not simply as a presentation tool, but as a way to increase student interaction, engagement, accessibility, and understanding.

In electronics, RF, and technology-based courses, many concepts are difficult to fully understand through lectures or textbooks alone. As a result, I intentionally integrate interactive technologies that allow students to visualize, manipulate, and experiment with concepts in real time. I regularly use tools such as Nearpod, PhET simulations, and Falstad Circuit Simulator to create lessons that are more interactive and exploration-driven.

Technology also allows me to support a wider range of learning styles and learning situations. Students can interact with simulations safely, repeat activities independently, and experiment without fear of damaging physical equipment. This is especially important when teaching complex electronics systems where visualization and guided exploration can significantly improve comprehension.

Another major area of growth has been the creation of instructional videos and digital learning resources. Using tools such as OBS, I create recorded demonstrations, walkthroughs, and guided tutorials that students can access outside of scheduled class time. These resources provide flexibility for students who may miss class, require additional review, or prefer learning at their own pace.

Over time, I have come to view educational technology as more than a convenience. When used effectively, it becomes a way to remove barriers, increase engagement, and create more opportunities for active learning. My approach focuses on using technology to complement hands-on instruction rather than replace it.

Looking forward, I plan to continue expanding my use of educational technology, particularly in areas involving interactive simulations, multimedia content creation, remote learning support, and technology-enhanced labs. My goal is to continue building learning environments where students can interact with technical concepts in meaningful, flexible, and engaging ways.

Competency 8

Professionalism, Development & Mentorship

I view teaching as an evolving professional practice that requires reflection, continuous learning, industry currency, and a willingness to support learners and colleagues.

Reflection

The rapid advancement of technology, particularly in areas such as artificial intelligence, embedded systems, automation, and software development, has significantly changed the expectations placed on technical educators. To remain effective as an instructor in electronics, RF, cybersecurity, and microcontroller-based systems, I have had to develop and maintain a strong system of continuous self-directed learning and professional development.

Much of my technical growth comes through independent study using online resources, forums, technical communities, documentation, and educational content platforms such as YouTube. These resources allow me to stay current with emerging technologies, industry trends, new development tools, and evolving teaching methods.

My approach to professional development closely mirrors the way I teach students. I learn best through hands-on experimentation, troubleshooting, building, and iterative discovery. As a result, I regularly spend time exploring and testing new technologies such as AI-based systems, AI cameras, microcontrollers, coding platforms, simulation tools, automation systems, and emerging educational technologies.

I also recognize that professional growth is strengthened through collaboration and knowledge sharing. As part of my mentorship approach, I do my best to support instructors who are interested in learning and improving their own technical and instructional practices. I regularly share tools, technologies, workflows, and instructional strategies that help improve lesson delivery, curriculum development, assessment processes, and grading efficiency.

Another important aspect of professionalism for me is maintaining adaptability in a rapidly changing technological landscape. Technical education is evolving quickly, and I believe instructors must remain curious, reflective, and willing to continuously learn alongside their students. Rather than viewing new technologies as disruptions, I try to approach them as opportunities to improve instruction, modernize learning experiences, and better prepare students for industry environments.

Looking forward, I intend to continue strengthening my professional practice through ongoing self-directed learning, experimentation with emerging technologies, collaboration with colleagues, and participation in curriculum modernization efforts. My goal is to remain an adaptable and forward-looking educator who can effectively support both students and fellow instructors in an increasingly technology-driven learning environment.
