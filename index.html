Competency 1

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is built on the idea that students learn best by actively engaging with real systems. In technical fields such as electronics, communications, microcontrollers, and cybersecurity, understanding comes from building, testing, failing, troubleshooting, and improving.

My approach is strongly influenced by three key educational role models: Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin. Each has shaped specific elements of my instructional practice.

Teaching Role Models

Richard Feynman

Richard Feynman was one of the most influential physicists and educators of the twentieth century. He received the 1965 Nobel Prize in Physics for his contributions to quantum electrodynamics, work that fundamentally changed how scientists understand the interaction between light and matter. Beyond his scientific achievements, Feynman became internationally respected for his extraordinary ability to explain highly complex ideas in ways that were engaging, understandable, and deeply human.

Feynman believed that true understanding was demonstrated not through the use of complicated language, but through the ability to explain difficult concepts clearly and simply. He relied heavily on analogies, storytelling, demonstrations, and real-world examples to make abstract scientific ideas accessible to learners.

Feynman is an important role model for my own teaching because his approach closely aligns with the way I strive to teach electronics and technology. Technical subjects can easily become overwhelming or overly theoretical, so I work to simplify concepts without removing their meaning.

His enthusiasm, curiosity, and excitement for discovery also strongly influence my classroom environment. Feynman demonstrated that learning science and technology should feel engaging, meaningful, and even fun. This reinforces my goal of helping students develop confidence, curiosity, and excitement about learning and problem-solving.

Fred Rogers

Fred Rogers was an American educator, television host, writer, musician, and ordained minister best known as the creator and host of Mister Rogers’ Neighborhood. Through his work in children’s education and public television, Rogers became one of the most respected and compassionate educators of the twentieth century. His program focused not only on teaching academic concepts, but also on emotional intelligence, empathy, communication, and helping people feel valued and understood.

Rogers believed that effective teaching begins with creating an environment where learners feel safe, respected, and supported. His calm communication style, thoughtful pacing, and genuine care for others demonstrated that learning is strongly connected to emotional well-being and confidence. He emphasized patience, reflection, kindness, and the importance of meeting learners where they are rather than where we expect them to be.

Fred Rogers is an important role model for my own teaching because his philosophy strongly influences the classroom environment I strive to create. Technical education can be intimidating, especially for students who may lack confidence or have struggled in traditional educational settings. Rogers’ approach reminds me that students learn best when they feel respected, encouraged, and safe asking questions or making mistakes.

His influence can be seen in the way I structure my classrooms and labs to be supportive, predictable, and student-centered. I aim to create learning environments where students feel comfortable experimenting, troubleshooting, and developing confidence through guided practice. Rogers demonstrated that kindness, patience, and empathy are not separate from effective teaching, but are essential parts of helping students grow and succeed.

Walter Lewin

Walter Lewin is a Dutch astrophysicist and former professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) who became internationally recognized for his dynamic and demonstration-driven physics lectures. During his decades of teaching at MIT, Lewin inspired millions of students through his ability to transform difficult scientific concepts into exciting and memorable learning experiences. His lectures became widely known through online educational platforms and are considered some of the most influential physics lectures ever recorded.

Lewin believed that science should not simply be explained, but experienced. His teaching style relied heavily on live demonstrations, visualization, prediction, and experimentation to help students directly observe physical principles in action. Rather than focusing only on equations and theory, he worked to create moments of curiosity and excitement that made abstract concepts feel real and meaningful.

Walter Lewin is an important role model for my own teaching because his instructional approach closely aligns with the hands-on and demonstration-based methods I use in electronics, RF systems, and technology education. Technical concepts often become far easier to understand when students can see systems operating in real time, make predictions, test ideas, and observe outcomes directly.

His influence can be seen in the way I design labs, demonstrations, and interactive learning activities that encourage students to actively engage with technical systems rather than passively observe them. Whether students are troubleshooting circuits, analyzing RF behavior, or working with microcontrollers, I aim to create learning experiences that connect theory to practical application in ways that are engaging, memorable, and meaningful.

Reflection

My teaching philosophy is the result of integrating the approaches of Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin into a cohesive instructional framework centered around clarity, engagement, hands-on learning, and student support.

When I first began teaching seven years ago, it was a career that I genuinely enjoyed. However, within a short period of time, I realized that helping people better themselves through learning technology was more than just a job for me—it was the reason I was meant to teach. Over the years, students and colleagues have consistently told me that the passion, excitement, and energy I bring into the classroom is something special. Seeing students accomplish things they never believed they could do, or witnessing the moment when a difficult concept suddenly “clicks” and understanding appears, has become one of the most rewarding parts of my career.

Feynman’s emphasis on clarity through simplification strongly shapes how I introduce new concepts. I aim to reduce complexity without removing meaning, using analogies, demonstrations, and structured explanations to help students build genuine understanding rather than rely on memorization. I believe a student truly understands a concept when they can explain it themselves and apply it in unfamiliar situations.

Rogers’ influence is reflected in the learning environment I strive to create. Technical education can be intimidating, especially for students who may lack confidence or have struggled in traditional learning environments. I prioritize creating a classroom culture that is respectful, predictable, supportive, and encouraging. I want students to feel safe asking questions, making mistakes, and learning through experimentation without fear of embarrassment or failure.

Lewin’s influence is evident in my use of demonstrations, visualization, and hands-on experiences. I regularly ask students to predict outcomes, test systems, and directly observe real-world results in labs and demonstrations. Whether students are working with RF systems, electronics circuits, microcontrollers, or simulations, I aim to create active learning experiences that connect theory to practical application in meaningful and memorable ways.

Overall, my instruction balances structure with flexibility, allowing me to deliver clear and technically rigorous content while adapting to student needs and supporting ongoing engagement. Together, these influences allow me to create learning experiences that focus not only on technical understanding, but also on confidence, curiosity, problem-solving, and helping students realize they are capable of more than they originally believed.

Competency 2

Instruction & Delivery

Effective delivery depends on how clearly information is structured, presented, and understood. I focus on multiple methods—visual, auditory, and hands-on—to support different learning styles and connect theory to practice.

Reflection

Reflection on Instruction and Delivery

Over the past seven years, my approach to instruction and delivery has evolved significantly through experience teaching electronics, RF systems, cybersecurity, microcontrollers, networking, and communications technology in both classroom and laboratory environments. Early in my teaching career, I focused heavily on delivering technical content accurately and effectively. Over time, however, I realized that strong instruction involves far more than simply transferring information. Effective teaching requires creating learning experiences that are engaging, accessible, hands-on, and meaningful to students.

One of the largest influences on my instructional growth has been recognizing how students learn technical concepts most effectively. In electronics and technology education, students often struggle when concepts remain abstract or disconnected from real-world application. As a result, I have increasingly focused on using demonstrations, guided experimentation, simulations, analogies, and hands-on labs to connect theory directly to practical experience.

My instructional style has been strongly influenced by educators such as Richard Feynman, Fred Rogers, and Walter Lewin. From Feynman, I have adopted the importance of clarity through simplification and using analogies to reduce complexity without removing meaning. From Rogers, I have learned the importance of creating respectful, supportive, and psychologically safe learning environments where students feel comfortable asking questions and making mistakes. From Lewin, I have embraced demonstration-driven instruction and the importance of creating memorable learning experiences through visualization and active participation.

Over time, I have also expanded the use of educational technology within my instruction. I regularly integrate simulation platforms such as PhET and Falstad Circuit Simulator, multimedia lessons, interactive activities, and recorded instructional videos into my courses. These tools allow students to explore technical concepts visually and interactively while also supporting students who require additional review or flexible learning opportunities.

The transition to online instruction during COVID further accelerated the evolution of my instructional approach. Teaching hands-on electronics and RF systems remotely required me to rethink how students could engage with technical material outside of a traditional lab environment. During this period, I developed online demonstrations, guided video lessons, simulation-based labs, and remote learning supports that allowed students to continue building practical understanding despite physical limitations. Many of these resources continue to be used today as supplemental learning tools.

Another major area of growth has been shifting toward more student-centered and guided learning approaches. Rather than focusing exclusively on lecture-based delivery, I increasingly structure labs and activities around guided discovery, troubleshooting, experimentation, and collaborative problem-solving. I want students to actively engage with systems, ask questions, test ideas, and build confidence through hands-on experience.

Looking back, my instruction and delivery have evolved from primarily focusing on content delivery toward creating complete learning experiences that balance technical rigor, accessibility, engagement, and practical application. My goal is not only to help students understand technology, but also to help them develop confidence, curiosity, and the belief that they are capable of solving complex problems and continuing to learn throughout their careers.

Competency 3

Assessment & Evaluation

My assessment philosophy focuses on evaluating both understanding and process. I design assessments that are authentic, clearly aligned with learning outcomes, and reflective of real-world technical problem-solving.

Reflection

One of the largest changes affecting assessment and evaluation in recent years has been the rapid growth of artificial intelligence tools and their use by students when completing academic work. This shift has required me to rethink how I assess student understanding, particularly in technical and hands-on courses where it is important to evaluate not only completed work, but also a student’s ability to explain, troubleshoot, and apply concepts independently.

As a result, my assessment strategies have evolved significantly. In my electronics, RF, and technology courses, many labs have shifted away from heavily report-based assessments and toward guided, hands-on learning activities that students complete directly in the lab environment. These labs are often structured more as competency-based or pass/fail activities focused on successful system operation, troubleshooting, and practical demonstration of skills.

A major change in my assessment approach has been the addition of instructor sign-offs throughout laboratory activities. During these checkpoints, students must physically demonstrate the operation of their systems and verbally explain how the circuit, program, or communication system functions. This allows me to directly evaluate student understanding, technical reasoning, troubleshooting ability, and engagement with the material in real time.

I have also reduced the emphasis on take-home reflection questions and written assessment sections within labs. Instead, many of these evaluation components have been moved into in-class, proctored quizzes and assessments where students must independently explain concepts, analyze systems, and apply technical knowledge without outside assistance. This change helps ensure that assessments more accurately reflect student understanding rather than the output of AI tools or external resources.

At the same time, I recognize that AI and modern technologies are becoming part of the professional and educational landscape. Rather than treating technology itself as the problem, I believe assessment design must evolve to focus more heavily on demonstration, application, communication, troubleshooting, and authentic technical performance. My goal is to create assessments that encourage learning, reinforce practical competency, and accurately measure a student’s understanding of the systems they are working with.

These changes have also reinforced the importance of continuous observation and interaction during labs and classroom activities. By engaging students in discussion, guided troubleshooting, demonstrations, and real-time questioning, I am better able to assess both technical competency and conceptual understanding while supporting students throughout the learning process.

Looking forward, I expect assessment and evaluation practices will continue to evolve alongside emerging technologies. My goal is to continue refining assessment methods that are fair, practical, industry-relevant, and focused on authentic demonstration of student learning and technical ability.

Competency 4

Diversity & Inclusion

I design my courses to reduce barriers and support diverse learners through Universal Design for Learning. My goal is to create environments where students can access content, engage meaningfully, and demonstrate understanding in multiple ways.

Reflection

Over the past several years, my understanding of diversity and inclusion in education has evolved significantly through both professional experience and personal reflection. As someone who experienced common learning disabilities and struggled within traditional educational environments, I understand firsthand how difficult learning can become when instruction is rigid, overly standardized, or disconnected from the needs of individual students.

These experiences have strongly influenced my approach to teaching. Rather than expecting every student to learn in the same way, my goal is to create an environment where students can use the tools, strategies, and learning methods that work best for them. I have increasingly focused on implementing Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles to reduce barriers and improve accessibility for all learners while still maintaining high academic standards.

In my electronics, RF, and technology classes, this has meant providing multiple methods of instruction and engagement. Students are supported through a combination of hands-on labs, guided demonstrations, written instructions, visual diagrams, video walkthroughs, simulations, and real-world examples. I recognize that students process information differently, and I aim to provide multiple pathways for understanding technical concepts.

I have also worked to create learning environments where students feel comfortable asking questions, making mistakes, and supporting one another. Teamwork and peer collaboration are strongly encouraged in my classrooms and labs because technical learning is often strengthened through discussion, troubleshooting, and shared problem solving. I believe students learn best when they feel respected, supported, and safe participating in the learning process.

My growth in applying diversity and inclusion has also involved becoming more proactive in assessment and course design. I now place greater emphasis on clear expectations, structured rubrics, scaffolded assignments, and flexibility in how students demonstrate their understanding. These strategies help reduce unnecessary barriers and allow students to focus on learning rather than simply navigating the educational process itself.

The integration of UDL principles into my teaching practice has become a major focus of my professional development and instructional philosophy. This growth is reflected in my collaboration with Accessibility Services and participation in the UDL pilot project within the Computer Automated Systems Technology program. The work has reinforced my belief that inclusive teaching does not lower standards; instead, it creates more opportunities for students to succeed and demonstrate their abilities. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Looking back, my own struggles as a learner have become one of the greatest strengths in my teaching practice. They have helped me develop empathy, flexibility, and a stronger understanding of how students experience learning challenges. My goal is to continue building classrooms that are engaging, accessible, technically rigorous, and supportive of the diverse needs and strengths that students bring to the learning environment.

Competency 5

Indigenization & Reconciliation

I am committed to integrating Indigenous perspectives into my teaching in ways that are authentic, respectful, and connected to practice—including recognizing the value of storytelling, relational learning, and community connection.

Reflection

Reflecting on Indigenous approaches to education has helped me recognize that storytelling, relationships, respect, and community connection are important parts of meaningful learning. These perspectives have encouraged me to think more carefully about how students experience the classroom environment and how learning is shaped not only by content, but also by culture, communication, and a sense of belonging.

One idea that has strongly influenced my thinking in this area comes from Fred Rogers. Rogers was deeply influenced by his beliefs as an ordained Presbyterian minister, yet on Mister Rogers’ Neighborhood he rarely promoted his religion directly or presented it as more important than the beliefs of others. Instead, he focused on quietly modeling the values that came from those beliefs through kindness, empathy, patience, listening, respect, and genuine care for people.

I believe this type of approach has important applications within modern Canadian education, particularly in multicultural classrooms where students may come from many different cultures, traditions, religions, and personal experiences. Rather than approaching Indigenization and reconciliation as something forced through ideology or political positioning, I believe there is value in modeling and reinforcing the underlying human values that support reconciliation: respect, relationship-building, listening, community, empathy, and recognition of different ways of knowing and learning.

In my own teaching practice, I aim to follow a similar approach by creating an environment built on respect, collaboration, support, and genuine care for students while remaining open and welcoming to diverse perspectives and experiences. I believe students are more likely to engage meaningfully when these values are demonstrated consistently through classroom culture and instructor behaviour rather than only being discussed theoretically.

Examples of this approach include encouraging collaborative troubleshooting during labs, creating classroom environments where students feel safe asking questions and making mistakes, and recognizing that students may approach learning and problem-solving differently based on their backgrounds and experiences. In technical education, I also use storytelling, demonstrations, and real-world examples to connect concepts to human experiences rather than presenting information only as isolated technical theory.

Another important part of this philosophy is allowing students multiple ways to engage with content through demonstrations, simulations, guided exploration, discussion, and hands-on activities. This supports different learning styles while reinforcing an environment where students feel respected and included regardless of background or previous experience.

This remains an area of ongoing growth and reflection for me. I recognize the importance of continuing to learn about Indigenous perspectives, histories, and educational approaches while finding authentic and respectful ways to incorporate these ideas into technical education. My goal is to continue building classrooms where students from all backgrounds feel respected, supported, and able to participate meaningfully in the learning process.

Competency 7

Technology

I integrate technology purposefully to improve engagement, support different learning styles, and connect classroom learning to real technical systems. Technology is selected based on its ability to improve learning, not simply because it is new.

Reflection

The integration of technology into education has become one of the strongest and fastest-growing areas of my teaching practice. Throughout my career, I have continually worked to improve my ability to use technology not simply as a presentation tool, but as a way to increase student interaction, engagement, accessibility, and understanding.

In electronics, RF, and technology-based courses, many concepts are difficult to fully understand through lectures or textbooks alone. As a result, I intentionally integrate interactive technologies that allow students to visualize, manipulate, and experiment with concepts in real time. I regularly use tools such as Nearpod, PhET simulations, and Falstad Circuit Simulator to create lessons that are more interactive and exploration-driven.

Technology also allows me to support a wider range of learning styles and learning situations. Students can interact with simulations safely, repeat activities independently, and experiment without fear of damaging physical equipment. This is especially important when teaching complex electronics systems where visualization and guided exploration can significantly improve comprehension.

Another major area of growth has been the creation of instructional videos and digital learning resources. Using tools such as OBS, I create recorded demonstrations, walkthroughs, and guided tutorials that students can access outside of scheduled class time. These resources provide flexibility for students who may miss class, require additional review, or prefer learning at their own pace.

Over time, I have come to view educational technology as more than a convenience. When used effectively, it becomes a way to remove barriers, increase engagement, and create more opportunities for active learning. My approach focuses on using technology to complement hands-on instruction rather than replace it.

Looking forward, I plan to continue expanding my use of educational technology, particularly in areas involving interactive simulations, multimedia content creation, remote learning support, and technology-enhanced labs. My goal is to continue building learning environments where students can interact with technical concepts in meaningful, flexible, and engaging ways.

Competency 8

Professionalism, Development & Mentorship

I view teaching as an evolving professional practice that requires reflection, continuous learning, industry currency, and a willingness to support learners and colleagues.

Reflection

The rapid advancement of technology, particularly in areas such as artificial intelligence, embedded systems, automation, and software development, has significantly changed the expectations placed on technical educators. To remain effective as an instructor in electronics, RF, cybersecurity, and microcontroller-based systems, I have had to develop and maintain a strong system of continuous self-directed learning and professional development.

Much of my technical growth comes through independent study using online resources, forums, technical communities, documentation, and educational content platforms such as YouTube. These resources allow me to stay current with emerging technologies, industry trends, new development tools, and evolving teaching methods.

My approach to professional development closely mirrors the way I teach students. I learn best through hands-on experimentation, troubleshooting, building, and iterative discovery. As a result, I regularly spend time exploring and testing new technologies such as AI-based systems, AI cameras, microcontrollers, coding platforms, simulation tools, automation systems, and emerging educational technologies.

I also recognize that professional growth is strengthened through collaboration and knowledge sharing. As part of my mentorship approach, I do my best to support instructors who are interested in learning and improving their own technical and instructional practices. I regularly share tools, technologies, workflows, and instructional strategies that help improve lesson delivery, curriculum development, assessment processes, and grading efficiency.

Another important aspect of professionalism for me is maintaining adaptability in a rapidly changing technological landscape. Technical education is evolving quickly, and I believe instructors must remain curious, reflective, and willing to continuously learn alongside their students. Rather than viewing new technologies as disruptions, I try to approach them as opportunities to improve instruction, modernize learning experiences, and better prepare students for industry environments.

Looking forward, I intend to continue strengthening my professional practice through ongoing self-directed learning, experimentation with emerging technologies, collaboration with colleagues, and participation in curriculum modernization efforts. My goal is to remain an adaptable and forward-looking educator who can effectively support both students and fellow instructors in an increasingly technology-driven learning environment.
